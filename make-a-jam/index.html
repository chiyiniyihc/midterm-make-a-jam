Pick a fruit for the main material, wash and peel them. Cut them into small pieces, remove the seeds and keep them aside in a large bowl or stack the fruit slices and cut them into small pieces.

Combine sugar with the chopped fruit and boil it. The amount of sugar depends on how sweet you would like your jam to be. The contents should boil the entire time.

Simmer until the jam has a thick consistency (about 30 minutes). Natural pectin in fruit helps the jam gel, but some fruits contain less than other. Add extra pectin if needed.

Let the jam cool for about 30-40 minutes and then store the jam in a glass jar.

Make sure you keep the jar at room temperature. Just spread the jam on your toast and enjoy!

Softer, delicate herbs were wonderful with strawberry jam; the woodier, more wintry herbs didn’t work at all. Highly recommend chopped mint, basil and lavender, which add surprising depth to a bowl of strawberries.

Orange jam has a warm and sunny flavor, and the addition of winter spices such as cinnamon, cloves and star anise makes it the perfect wintertime jam. Add just a tiny bit of spice without overpowering the orange.

Blueberry Jam wins the award for most versatile; it worked wonderfully with everything but mint. Lavender lightly perfumed the berries and made them sweeter somehow.

Lemon Marmalade comes with lemon fiber and pulps. It preserved with fructose and real fruit juice so that the strong fragrance goes well with cinnamon, cardamon, rosemary or vanilla.

  • Stone Age People discovered ways to preserve food for storage, to make survival easier when food was scarce.
  • Ancient Greeks Preservation using honey or sugar was one common method and the Ancient Greeks also used to use honey to preserve quince. Syrups made from honey and sugar were also used to preserve food; honey has no moisture so it preserves any food encased within it.
  • 4th century AD The first written recipe for jam is recorded in the first known cookbook; De Re Coquinaria (The Art of Cooking) written by Marcus Gavius Apicius.
  • CRUSADES Soldiers brought back sugar from the Middle East to Western Europe, significantly boosting jam’s popularity.
  • 16th century The French astrologer and physician Nostradamus was another famous lover of jam, so much so that he even wrote a treatise on it. His ‘Treatise on Makeup and Jam’ appeared in 1555. It contains recipes for cherry jam, quince jelly and pear preserve, as well as recipes to cure the plague or even attract love.
  • 17th century Sugar still wasn’t cheap during this period, jam was a way for people to display their wealth. Louis XIV was said to be so fond of jam that he insisted it be brought out to finish every meal.
  • 18th century In 1785, Napoleon offered a reward to anyone who could find a way to preserve large quantities of food for soldiers on the move. Inventor Nicholas Appert discovered that boiling food at high temperatures and sealing it in an airtight container kept it safe. Jam was brought to the USA from Europe by John Chapman.
  • 19th century Jerome Monroe Smucker from Ohio opened a cider mill in 1897 with fruit from trees that Chapman had planted. After a few years, he and his wife began to make apple butter (a type of sweet apple sauce made using sugar).
  • 20th century In 1918, the American company Welch’s began producing its first jam product, Grapelade. The entire inventory was bought by the US army and shipped to troops during the First World War.
  • WORLD WAR II Preservation centres were set up in farm kitchens, village halls and even sheds, largely run by volunteers from the community. Between 1940 and 1945, over 5,300 tons of fruit were preserved and turned into over 1,600 tons of jam.
